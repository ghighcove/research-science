Key Findings

Headline: Fluvoxamine × Rhabdomyolysis — PRR = 826
Patients on fluvoxamine are reported to FAERS with myoglobin in urine (a rhabdomyolysis marker) at 826× the background rate (n=63, chi²=51,114). A coherent cluster: CPK elevated (PRR=17.4), LDH elevated (PRR=17.6), troponin elevated (PRR=37.4), and antipsychotic drug level increased (PRR=71.9) — consistent with CYP1A2 drug-drug interactions causing statin or antipsychotic accumulation to toxic levels. Requires clinical pharmacologist review.
Serotonin Syndrome: Consistent High Signal Across All 6 Drugs
All 6 SSRIs show serotonin syndrome as a top novel signal (PRR 15–41), representing a post-market adverse event that clinical trial profiles systematically undercount due to exclusion of polypharmacy patients from RCTs. This signal is consistent with existing FDA warnings but quantifies its real-world magnitude for the first time in this comparative format.
QT Prolongation: Citalopram and Escitalopram Lead (Validation Signal)
Citalopram (PRR=9.15, n=1,750) and Escitalopram (PRR=8.87, n=1,129) show the strongest QT prolongation signals — consistent with the FDA 2011 safety communication limiting citalopram doses. This validates the pipeline methodology: it recapitulates known, independently confirmed findings.

Methodology

Data Source

FDA FAERS (Adverse Event Reporting System) via openFDA public API. Covers 2004–present; voluntary adverse event reports submitted by patients, providers, and manufacturers. Total: 20,006,989 reports as of March 2026.

Signal Criteria

Evans et al. standard pharmacovigilance criteria: PRR ≥ 2.0, count ≥ 3, chi² ≥ 4.0. PRR is computed as (a/N_drug) / (N_event/N_total) using 2×2 contingency table.

Comparison Baseline

"Known" AEs are compared against a conservative set of ~20 events from published SSRI clinical trial adverse event profiles (nausea, insomnia, headache, dry mouth, sexual dysfunction, etc.). Events not in this list are flagged as "novel" — meaning post-market signal not prominently captured in registration-era trial data.

Limitations

FAERS captures reports, not causation. High PRR = disproportionate reporting, not proven harm. Notable biases: notoriety bias (publicized effects are over-reported), Weber effect, indication bias (SSRIs prescribed for depression — baseline suicide risk is elevated), and co-medication confounding.
